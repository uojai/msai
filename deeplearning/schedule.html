Convolutional Neural Networks – The Eyes of AI

This lecture dives into the "eyes" of artificial intelligence by exploring Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), the specialized architectures that allow machines to interpret the visual world. We shift from general-purpose networks to models specifically designed to handle the high-dimensional complexity and spatial relationships of raw pixel matrices. Students will trace the hierarchical journey of an image—from biological inspiration to the mathematical application of convolutional filters and pooling—learning how these layers automatically extract features like edges, textures, and complex structural shapes. By the end of this session, you will understand the structural mechanics of feature learning and how to build a CNN from scratch to recognize and classify images with high precision.

Topics: Convolution, Max Pooling, CNN Architecture, Hyperparameters

Week 3

January 26, 2026

Training Neural Networks

This lecture provides a comprehensive foundation in the mechanics of neural network training, moving beyond architecture to explore how machines actually learn from data. Students will trace the iterative cycle of learning, beginning with Forward-propagation to generate predictions and the use of Mean Squared Error (MSE) to quantify performance. We delve into the calculus of Back-propagation and the Chain Rule to understand how errors are attributed to specific weights, followed by the application of Gradient Descent to minimize those errors. The session also addresses critical practical challenges in deep learning, including the selection of Learning Rates, the advantages of Mini-batch and Adaptive optimizers like Adam, and essential regularization techniques such as Dropout and Early Stopping to prevent overfitting and ensure models generalize effectively to real-world data.

Artificial Neural Networks

This lecture offers a compelling exploration of the bridge between biological intelligence and artificial neural architectures, providing a foundational understanding of how Deep Learning models are built and optimized. Students will trace the evolution of neural networks starting from the biological blueprint of the human brain—examining how dendrites, synapses, and axons translate into mathematical inputs, weights, and activation functions. The session moves from the fundamental Perceptron to the complexity of Multilayer Perceptrons (MLPs) and Deep Neural Networks (DNNs), illustrating how stacking layers and applying non-linear functions like ReLU and Sigmoid allow machines to solve complex problems. By the end of the lecture, students will understand the structural mechanics of "deep" layers and how loss functions like Binary Cross-Entropy and Mean Squared Error are used to measure and refine a model’s predictive power.

Introduction and Logistics

This introductory lecture provides a foundational overview of Deep Learning, exploring its transformative impact across fields like computer vision, autonomous systems, and natural language processing. We begin by tracing the architecture of Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs) back to their simplest computational unit: the Perceptron, a basic linear classifier designed to mimic biological neuron functions. The session then details the transition to Multilayer Perceptrons (MLPs), illustrating how stacking layers of neurons and incorporating non-linear activation functions allow these networks to learn complex patterns within high-dimensional data.
